Interview de Chris Matthieu, créateur de Nodester

Catégorie : Interview | 23 mars 2011 | Tags :  Node.js, Nodester

La sérié d’articles sur Node.JS est terminée (intro, Cloud9, première application et Nodester).

Pendant que je préparais ces articles, j’ai pu discuter avec plusieurs personnes de la communauté Node. Et je vous propose une petite interview de Chris Matthieu, créateur de Nodester, plateforme d’hébergement gratuite d’applications Node. Il est aussi le Directeur général de GetVocal (surf the Web with your voice).

C’est parti !

 

Web Tambouille : Why did you create Nodester ?

Chris Matthieu : I created Nodester over the holidays (on my vacation) because I got tired of waiting for the other Node.JS hosting providers to send me an invitation. It turned out to be a great opportunity to learn more about the underpinnings of Node.JS and at the same time create an open source project that all Node.JS developers could use to host their own applications.

 

WT : Why are you a big fan of Node.JS ?

CM : I think that Node.JS is revolutionary. The event-driven architecture makes Node.JS perfect for the real-time web and mobile apps of today. I believe that we are going to see much innovation being driven by Node.JS real-time apps. I also believe that Javascript on the client and server also have huge potential for allowing developers to focus on a single language. I am starting to see a trend where more new apps today behave like a single web page entirely powered by jQuery AJAX and websocket browser pushes. We will see many more of these apps come to market this year and it will become the new norm and expectation within 2-3 years from now.

 

WT : What is your main goal with Nodester ? Become the Heroku of Node.JS ?

CM : We accomplished our initial goals of the project as most of the other Node.JS hosting providers have now made commitments to offer smaller application hosting. I like to like that we disrupted a market. We have also emplowered many developers and even several companies to run Nodester in their datacenters and on public clouds including: Amazon, Rackspace, and GoGrid. We are currently offering free Node.JS hosting on our open source platform running in Amazone EC2 through grants provided by Tropo (http://tropo.com), the cloud communications company. We plan to charge for hosting larger applications in the future as well as for professional services and yes, become the Heroku of Node.JS hosting services. I like the open source business model very much like MySQL and SugarCRM. We are establishing trust and giving back to the community.

 

WT : Main features in the next releases ?

CM : We have new updates for Nodester releasing every couple of days! We now have a core team that consists of 4 developers and we are looking for more help. We also have many contributors adding value to the project every day by submitting pull requests to our open source platform on GitHub (https://github.com/nodester). I just heard about one developer working on a Nodester dashboard using our RESTful APIs to make administration even easier. I also know of a Linux admin that is working on an installation package that will make it easy to deploy Nodester on any Linux machine ! This will allow us to bring Node.JS hosting to the masses to further disrupt the traditional hosting market !

 

WT : Something special to say ?

CM : I would like to give Ryan Dahl props for creating Node.JS and standing up for what he believes in and taking his Node.JS talks on the road. I would like to give Joyent credit for supporting him in his efforts even though they appear to have mostly monetary reasons for doing so. I would also like to give kudos to the Node.JS community for being quick to innovate and build an ecosystem that rivals Ruby on Rails in the early days. There are many bright and talented engineers already on the Node.JS bandwagon because they see the promise of the technology. Everyone else should start learning Javascript today !

 

WT : What is your favorite dessert ?

CM : I love desserts  Creme Brules are my favorite. We usually order one from new restaurants my family visits just so we can compare them to others we have tried over the years at other restaurants. Being French Cajun myself, I will also always go out of my way to find restaurants that serve Cafe DuMonde-style beignets (http://www.cafedumonde.com/). Beignets were brought to Louisiana by the Acadians. These were fried fritters, sometimes filled with fruit. Today, the beignet is a square piece of dough, fried and covered with powdered sugar and served warm. OMG, that makes me hungry for one :)
